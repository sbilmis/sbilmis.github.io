TÜBİTAK among Europe's research organisations

As a research performer, TÜBİTAK published 1,577 journal articles in 2020–2025 — the smallest in this set of 17 European research organisations, and just 0.4% of Türkiye's national output. Its performing arm is spread across 15 institutes with ~3,264 researchers. Across Europe the funding agency and the research-performing organisation are separate bodies, and performing institutes sit under one umbrella — the structure that builds scale.

3 · Researchers — and the applied-mission undercount

Journal articles per researcher, 2020–25. TÜBİTAK is lowest here (0.5 vs an applied-RTO median of 1.3) — so it is smaller on both headcount and per-head journal output. That per-head figure should be read with care: TÜBİTAK's institutes are applied and mission bodies — defence (SAGE), cyber-security (BİLGEM), metrology (UME), space (UZAY), HPC/IT services (ULAKBİM) — whose output is largely patents, standards, prototypes and classified work that journal bibliometrics do not count. The policy point stands either way: a dedicated research-performing organisation — with a research mission, more researchers, and a single identity — is what converts effort into measured output. (Per-head figures are approximate; staff definitions vary and Helmholtz is excluded as its inputs cover the whole association.)

4 · Quality — where TÜBİTAK stands

Citations-per-paper (x) vs Q1-journal share (y); bubble size = article volume. TÜBİTAK trails on both (CPP 11.9, Q1 49%), though it is closing fast (its Q1 share rose 49→57% and open access 31→52% across 2020–25).

Mega-collaboration (CERN/LHC) papers. Hyper-authorship papers (≥100 authors) are attributed to every participating organisation and carry huge citations. They inflate the physics-heavy performers far more than TÜBİTAK (which barely participates): removing them cuts Demokritos' citations-per-paper 19.9→14.4, CEA 20.7→17.8 and Max Planck 26.0→23.7, while TÜBİTAK is unchanged (11.9→11.8). 5 · Scenario: a unified, scaled performer

An illustrative scale-to-parity scenario (not a forecast). If TÜBİTAK's institutes were consolidated under one research organisation — separate from the funding council — and grew to the applied-RTO peer-median share of national output:

8,133 articles / 6 yr · ≈5.2× today
Applied-RTO peers (Fraunhofer, VTT, SINTEF, TNO, imec, AIT, RISE) sit at a median 2.2% of national output. Applied to Türkiye's 364,706 articles (2020–25), that is ~8,133 vs TÜBİTAK's current 1,577. Assumption: reach the applied-RTO median national share; holds field/quality constant. Illustrative of the scale on the table, not a prediction.
Why unification helps beyond headcount: a single umbrella (like CNRS's one profile at 44% of France) concentrates attribution, visibility and ranking that TÜBİTAK's 15 separately-indexed institutes currently fragment — before a single extra researcher is hired.

Methodology & caveats
How this is built

Bibliometrics (articles, CPP, Q1, OA, international share) are from our own frozen Scopus extraction, per organisation via affiliation-string queries with a country filter (2020–2025 journal articles; Q1 from SCImago SJR). Counts are approximate where an organisation's name is a short token or where legal entities changed (RISE 2016 merger, AIT rename); Helmholtz is a partial capture — only centres whose affiliation carries the 'Helmholtz' token (DLR/Jülich/DESY/KIT excluded).

Structure (national funder, funder≠performer, institute counts) and researchers are compiled from each organisation's public reports and official pages; researcher figures use each body's research-staff definition and are best estimates, not strictly comparable FTE. Do not compute per-FTE for Helmholtz.

TÜBİTAK as performer: the bibliometrics measure only TÜBİTAK's research-performing institutes. TÜBİTAK is 71% funder / 29% performer by expenditure — the funder arm is not counted here. Its 15 institutes carry separate Scopus identities (companion TÜBİTAK institutes dashboard).

The scenario is illustrative, holding quality/field constant and assuming the applied-RTO peer-median national share — it shows the order of magnitude at stake under consolidation, not a prediction.
